Define Success — SMART Metrics 🎯

"The AI should work well" is not a goal. Learn to define success in numbers, at three levels: model, system, and business.

Step 1 — What makes a goal SMART? (10 min)

A SMART goal is Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound. Compare:

❌ Not SMART✅ SMARTWhat changed?
"The model should be accurate""Achieve at least 85% accuracy on the test set within 3 months"Number + deadline
"The system should be fast""Return results in under 2 seconds for 95% of requests by launch"Threshold + coverage + deadline
"It should help the business""Reduce missed payments by 12% within 3 months of go-live"Business number + timeframe

Step 2 — The three levels of AI metrics (10 min)

LevelMeasures…Typical metrics
ModelIs the AI's prediction good?Accuracy, precision, recall, F1, false positive/negative rate
SystemDoes the software around the model perform?Response time, uptime, throughput/scalability
BusinessDoes it create real value?Cost savings, revenue, customer satisfaction, task reduction %

Worked example: food-delivery ETA predictor

  • Model: "Predict delivery time within ±5 minutes for 80% of orders by end of month 2."
  • System: "Show the ETA in the app in under 1 second for 99% of orders at launch."
  • Business: "Cut 'where is my order?' support tickets by 30% within 3 months."

💡 Notice the chainA good model (±5 min) enables a good system (instant ETA), which delivers business value (fewer complaints). If you can't draw this chain, the metric probably doesn't matter.

🏆 Exercise — the loan-risk early-warning system (25 min, in pairs)

Scenario: a bank wants AI to predict which customers are likely to miss their monthly loan payment, so customer-service teams can proactively reach out and offer help before the payment is missed.

Your task: define how success will be measured. Write 3 model metrics, 3 system metrics, and 3 business metrics — each as a full SMART goal sentence.

Follow this recipe for every metric:

  1. Pick the metric (e.g., "false positive rate")
  2. Add a number (target or threshold) — make it achievable, not fantasy
  3. Add a timeframe ("within 3 months", "during the pilot", "by launch")
  4. Sanity-check: is it relevant to the bank's goal of preventing missed payments?

Hints per level:

  • Model: think about correct predictions, false alarms (annoying calls to safe customers!), and missed risky customers (the expensive mistake).
  • System: the service team opens a dashboard every morning — what must be true about speed, availability, and capacity?
  • Business: what would the bank's CFO want to see change? Missed payments, outreach volume, collection costs…

Self-check before revealing: read each goal aloud and ask: Specific? Measurable? Achievable? Relevant? Time-bound? All five, or rewrite it.

Your numbers don't have to match these — what matters is that every goal has a metric, a number, and a deadline, and sits at the right level.

💡 DiscussionWhich is worse here: a false positive (calling a customer who was fine) or a false negative (missing a customer who defaults)? How should that change your model targets? There's no single right answer — argue it!
