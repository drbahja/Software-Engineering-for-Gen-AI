The AI Lifecycle in Action 🩺

Before writing any code, learn to think like an AI engineer: plan a complete AI project, step by step.

Warm-up (5 min, in pairs)

AI is not only robots and sci-fi movies — it is everywhere. Name 3 AI applications you have already encountered today. (Hint: your phone unlocked itself somehow. Your feed chose what to show you. Your map found the fastest route.)

The 7 steps of every AI project

Every real AI system — from self-driving cars to fraud detection — goes through the same lifecycle:

  1. Problem Scoping — what are we solving, and why does it matter?
  2. Data Acquisition — what data do we need, and where does it come from?
  3. Data Exploration — understand, clean, and prepare the data
  4. Modeling — choose and train a model
  5. Model Evaluation — measure with the right metrics
  6. Deployment — put the model where users need it
  7. Maintenance & Monitoring — keep it working as the world changes

💡 Reference: self-driving carsIn the lecture we applied all 7 steps to a self-driving car (cameras + LiDAR data → CNNs → recall on safety-critical objects → deployment on an in-car GPU → retraining on edge cases). Use that example as your model when working below.

🏆 Exercise — plan a diabetes-risk AI system

You are responsible for designing an AI system that predicts whether a patient is at risk of developing diabetes, based on their health data. Apply the 7 lifecycle steps to plan this project.

For each step, write your answer in one or two sentences. Think critically: what actions would you take, what information do you need, and how does each decision support building a reliable, real-world system?

  1. Problem Scoping: What is the problem, and why is it important?
  2. Data Acquisition: What data would you collect?
  3. Data Exploration: How would you understand and prepare it?
  4. Modeling: What kind of model could predict diabetes risk?
  5. Model Evaluation: Which metrics, and why?
  6. Deployment: Where and how would the model run?
  7. Maintenance & Monitoring: How do you keep it reliable over time?

Deliverable: a one-page plan (7 short answers). Be ready to present one step to the class in 1 minute.

🔓 Reveal the model answer (only after you finish!)
StepModel answer
1. Problem ScopingPredict diabetes risk so doctors can intervene early and improve patient outcomes.
2. Data AcquisitionAge, gender, BMI, blood pressure, glucose and insulin levels, physical activity, family history.
3. Data ExplorationHandle missing values, outliers, and class imbalance; visualise relationships (e.g., glucose vs. diabetes); clean the data.
4. ModelingA classification model: logistic regression, decision tree, or random forest.
5. Model EvaluationAccuracy, precision, recall, F1 — with special weight on recall, to avoid missing high-risk patients.
6. DeploymentInside the hospital's Electronic Health Record system, giving doctors real-time risk predictions.
7. MaintenanceMonitor performance, retrain with new data, watch for model drift, check fairness across patient groups.
