Learn Anything with NotebookLM 📚

Turn AI into your personal research assistant — and teach yourself today's most valuable AI skill: prompt engineering.

What is NotebookLM?

NotebookLM (notebooklm.google.com) is a free AI research and learning tool from Google. The key difference from a normal chatbot: it only answers from the sources YOU give it — so its answers are grounded, with citations you can click and check. No sources, no answer, no hallucinated "facts".

What it can do

  • Upload sources — PDFs, Google Docs, web links, pasted text, even YouTube videos
  • Ask grounded questions — every answer cites the exact passage it came from
  • Study guide & FAQ — one click turns your sources into revision material
  • Audio Overview — generates a podcast-style conversation about your sources (two AI hosts discussing your topic!)
  • Mind map — a visual, clickable map of the concepts in your sources
  • Briefing doc & summaries — condense many documents into one overview

⚠️ Access note (China)NotebookLM is a Google service and needs the classroom VPN. If it is unreachable from your seat, work in pairs with someone who has access, or follow the instructor's projector demo — the exercise below still works.

Today's topic: Prompt Engineering

Prompt engineering is the skill of writing instructions that get the best results from AI models. It matters because the same model can give a useless or a brilliant answer — depending only on how you ask. You'll use it every day of this course (and your career).

The five techniques that matter most:

TechniqueWhat it meansExample
Give a roleTell the AI who to be"You are a patient teacher explaining to a beginner…"
Be specificSay exactly what you want, and for whom"…in 3 short paragraphs, for a student with no coding background"
Set the formatAsk for a table, list, steps…"Answer as a table with columns X and Y"
Show examplesGive one sample of a good answer"Like this: [example]. Now do the same for…"
Ask step by stepRequest reasoning before the answer"Think through it step by step, then conclude"

🏆 Exercise — teach yourself prompt engineering, using AI

Your mission: build a NotebookLM notebook that teaches you prompt engineering — practising prompt engineering while you do it. Learning the skill with the skill!

  1. Create a notebook at notebooklm.google.com called "Prompt Engineering".
  2. Add 2–4 sources. Good ones to search for: Google's "Prompt engineering guide", OpenAI's "Prompt engineering best practices", Anthropic's prompt engineering docs, or any good article/YouTube video on prompt engineering you find.
  3. Design your learning scenario. Write down (in one sentence) who you are and what you want to achieve, e.g. "I'm a beginner who wants to write better prompts for study help."
  4. Write 5 prompts to ask your notebook — each one must use a different technique from the table above. Ideas:
    • "You are a tutor for beginners. Explain prompt engineering in 3 simple paragraphs." (role + specific)
    • "Make a table of the 5 most important prompting techniques with one example each." (format)
    • "Here is my prompt: 'write about AI'. Rewrite it 3 ways to get much better answers, and explain why each is better." (examples)
    • "Step by step, how should I build a prompt for summarising a long report?" (step by step)
    • "Create a 5-question quiz to test whether I understood the sources." (format + specific)
  5. Try the magic buttons: generate a Study Guide, then an Audio Overview — listen to the two hosts discuss YOUR sources.

Deliverable: your scenario sentence, your 5 prompts, and the single most surprising thing you learned. Be ready to share one prompt with the class — and say which technique made it work.

💡 Extra scenarios where NotebookLM shinesRevising a course (upload lecture notes → study guide + quiz) · understanding a research paper (upload PDF → "explain like I'm 15") · comparing documents ("what do source 1 and source 2 disagree about?") · meeting prep (upload reports → briefing doc) · learning on the go (Audio Overview during your commute).
